## 1. Welcome & Platform Overview

### Welcome to Tomorrow's Earth Expo 2026

Welcome! Tomorrow's Earth Expo (TEE) 2026 is a comprehensive sustainability innovation platform designed to empower high school students to develop, submit, and showcase real-world environmental solutions. As a teacher facilitator, you play a crucial role in guiding students through this transformative learning experience.

### Your Role as a Teacher Facilitator

Your responsibilities extend beyond traditional grading. You are a mentor, guide, and evaluator who helps students develop critical thinking, scientific reasoning, and professional problem-solving skills. Throughout the platform, you will:

**Guide Student Learning:** Help students understand the platform, navigate project development, and overcome challenges. Your guidance should encourage independent thinking while providing structure and support.

**Evaluate Projects:** Review student submissions using provided rubrics, assess the quality of their analysis and solutions, and provide constructive feedback that drives improvement.

**Facilitate Collaboration:** Support team dynamics, help resolve conflicts, and ensure all team members contribute meaningfully to the project.

**Connect to Curriculum:** Integrate the expo into your classroom learning, using projects to reinforce sustainability concepts and environmental science principles.

**Inspire Innovation:** Create an environment where students feel safe taking intellectual risks, experimenting with solutions, and learning from failures.

### Platform Structure for Teachers

The platform provides several key areas where you interact with students and manage their projects:

**Teacher Dashboard:** Your central hub for managing all student projects, providing feedback, and tracking progress. Access this from the main navigation when logged in as a teacher.

**Student Projects:** View all projects submitted by your students, organized by status (Draft, Submitted, Under Review, Approved, Rejected). Each project shows team members, submission date, and current status.

**Feedback System:** Provide written feedback, rubric scores, and approval/rejection decisions directly through the platform. Students receive notifications when you provide feedback.

**Project Approval Workflow:** Review projects before they appear on the Innovation Hub. Your approval is required for projects to become public and eligible for voting.

**Analytics & Reporting:** View class-level analytics showing project submission rates, category distribution, and student engagement metrics.

## 2. Project Evaluation & Rubrics

### Understanding the Evaluation Framework

The Tomorrow's Earth Expo uses a comprehensive rubric-based evaluation system designed to assess multiple dimensions of student work. This approach ensures fair, consistent evaluation while providing students with clear feedback on their performance.

### Core Evaluation Dimensions

#### 1. Problem Identification & Analysis (25%)

This dimension assesses how well students understand and articulate the environmental problem they're addressing.

**Excellent (90-100%):** Students clearly identify a specific, well-defined environmental problem. They provide compelling evidence of the problem's significance through data, research, and stakeholder impact analysis. The problem statement is sophisticated and demonstrates deep understanding of root causes and contributing factors.

**Proficient (80-89%):** Students identify a clear environmental problem with adequate supporting evidence. The problem statement is specific and demonstrates good understanding of the issue. Some analysis of causes and impacts is present.

**Developing (70-79%):** Students identify an environmental problem but may lack specificity or sufficient supporting evidence. The problem statement is present but could be more clearly articulated. Limited analysis of causes and impacts.

**Beginning (Below 70%):** The environmental problem is vague or poorly defined. Minimal evidence or research supports the problem statement. Limited understanding of the issue demonstrated.

**Evaluation Questions to Consider:**
- Is the problem clearly defined and specific?
- Does the student provide sufficient evidence of the problem's significance?
- Is there analysis of root causes and contributing factors?
- Does the student demonstrate understanding of who is affected by this problem?

#### 2. Solution Development & Innovation (25%)

This dimension evaluates the quality, creativity, and feasibility of the proposed solution.

**Excellent (90-100%):** The solution is innovative, well-developed, and clearly addresses the identified problem. Students demonstrate creative thinking while maintaining scientific rigor. The solution is feasible and includes specific implementation details. Consideration of scalability and long-term sustainability is evident.

**Proficient (80-89%):** The solution is well-developed and addresses the problem effectively. Good use of scientific principles and practical considerations. Implementation plan is clear with reasonable detail. Some consideration of scalability.

**Developing (70-79%):** The solution addresses the problem but may lack innovation or detail. Implementation plan is present but could be more specific. Limited consideration of feasibility or scalability.

**Beginning (Below 70%):** The solution is vague or doesn't clearly address the problem. Implementation plan is unclear or unrealistic. Little consideration of feasibility.

**Evaluation Questions to Consider:**
- Does the solution directly address the identified problem?
- Is the solution innovative while remaining grounded in science?
- Is the implementation plan specific and realistic?
- Has the student considered scalability and long-term impacts?
- Are resources and constraints realistically assessed?

#### 3. Data Analysis & Evidence (20%)

This dimension assesses the quality of research, data collection, and use of evidence to support decisions.

**Excellent (90-100%):** Students use multiple credible sources and synthesize information effectively. Data analysis is sophisticated, showing understanding of patterns, trends, and implications. Evidence is used strategically to support all major claims. Sources are properly cited.

**Proficient (80-89%):** Students use credible sources and conduct adequate data analysis. Evidence supports major claims. Sources are cited. Some synthesis of information from multiple sources.

**Developing (70-79%):** Students use some credible sources but analysis may be superficial. Evidence is present but may not fully support all claims. Citation is inconsistent.

**Beginning (Below 70%):** Limited use of credible sources. Minimal data analysis. Evidence is weak or missing. Poor or no citation.

**Evaluation Questions to Consider:**
- Are sources credible and appropriate?
- Is data analysis thorough and accurate?
- Does evidence support the student's claims?
- Are sources properly cited?
- Has the student considered contradictory evidence?

#### 4. Reflection & Learning (15%)

This dimension evaluates the student's ability to reflect on their process, learning, and outcomes.

**Excellent (90-100%):** Student demonstrates sophisticated reflection on their learning process, challenges overcome, and insights gained. They thoughtfully consider what worked, what didn't, and why. Connections to broader environmental and professional concepts are clear.

**Proficient (80-89%):** Student reflects meaningfully on their process and learning. They identify challenges and successes. Some connection to broader concepts.

**Developing (70-79%):** Student provides basic reflection but may lack depth. Some identification of challenges and learning.

**Beginning (Below 70%):** Minimal or superficial reflection. Limited identification of learning or challenges.

**Evaluation Questions to Consider:**
- Does the student demonstrate genuine reflection on their work?
- Can they articulate what they learned?
- Do they identify challenges and how they overcame them?
- Can they connect their learning to broader concepts?
- Is there evidence of growth mindset?

#### 5. Communication & Presentation (15%)

This dimension assesses how clearly and professionally students communicate their work.

**Excellent (90-100%):** Communication is clear, organized, and professional. Visual presentations are effective and enhance understanding. Writing is well-organized with proper grammar and vocabulary. Explanations are clear and compelling.

**Proficient (80-89%):** Communication is clear and generally well-organized. Visuals are appropriate. Writing is mostly clear with minor errors. Explanations are adequate.

**Developing (70-79%):** Communication is present but may lack clarity or organization. Some visual or written errors. Explanations could be clearer.

**Beginning (Below 70%):** Communication is unclear or poorly organized. Significant errors in writing or presentation. Explanations are difficult to follow.

**Evaluation Questions to Consider:**
- Is the project well-organized and easy to follow?
- Are visuals clear and appropriate?
- Is the writing clear and professional?
- Are explanations compelling and understandable?
- Does the presentation enhance understanding of the project?

### Using the Rubric Effectively

**Before Evaluation:** Review the rubric carefully and familiarize yourself with each dimension and performance level. Consider the context of your students' experience and adjust expectations accordingly while maintaining rigor.

**During Evaluation:** Score each dimension independently, then provide an overall score. Use specific examples from the student work to justify your scores. Avoid letting one strong dimension influence scores in other areas.

**Providing Scores:** Use the percentage scale (0-100%) or the performance level descriptions. Be consistent in your scoring approach across all students and projects.

**Justifying Scores:** Always explain your scoring with specific evidence from the student work. Reference the rubric criteria and provide examples of what the student did well and where they can improve.

## 3. Providing Effective Feedback

### The Purpose of Feedback

Effective feedback is one of the most powerful tools for student learning. Your feedback should be specific, actionable, and focused on helping students improve their work and thinking. Feedback is most effective when it:

**Identifies Strengths:** Recognizes what students did well and why it was effective. This builds confidence and helps students understand what good work looks like.

**Addresses Specific Areas for Improvement:** Points out particular aspects that need development with concrete suggestions for how to improve.

**Connects to Learning Goals:** Links feedback to the project objectives and broader learning outcomes.

**Encourages Reflection:** Asks questions that prompt students to think more deeply about their work.

**Maintains a Supportive Tone:** Balances constructive criticism with encouragement, maintaining student motivation and confidence.

### Feedback Framework

Use this framework to structure your feedback for maximum impact:

#### Step 1: Acknowledge Effort and Strengths

Begin by recognizing what students did well. Be specific about which aspects of their work were strong and why.

**Example:** "Your problem identification is excellent. You clearly defined the issue, provided compelling statistics about plastic waste in our region, and explained how it affects both marine ecosystems and human communities. This demonstrates thorough research and critical thinking."

#### Step 2: Identify Key Areas for Improvement

Focus on 1-3 major areas where the student can improve. Prioritize feedback on the most important aspects that will have the greatest impact on learning.

**Example:** "Your solution is creative, but it needs more detail about implementation. Specifically, explain how you would train community members to use the new recycling system and how you would ensure long-term participation."

#### Step 3: Provide Specific Suggestions

Don't just identify problems; suggest concrete ways to address them. Give students actionable steps they can take to improve.

**Example:** "To strengthen your data analysis, consider including a comparison of your proposed solution with similar programs in other communities. This would help demonstrate that your approach is realistic and evidence-based."

#### Step 4: Ask Reflective Questions

Pose questions that encourage students to think more deeply about their work. These questions should guide them toward better solutions without simply giving them the answers.

**Example:** "What would happen if your proposed solution encountered resistance from community members? How would you address this challenge? What would you do differently?"

#### Step 5: Provide Encouragement

End with encouragement and confidence in the student's ability to improve. Connect their work to the broader purpose of the expo.

**Example:** "Your passion for this environmental issue is evident in your work. With these revisions, your project will be a powerful contribution to the Innovation Hub and could inspire other students to take action on this issue."

### Types of Feedback

**Approval Feedback:** When a project meets standards and is ready for the Innovation Hub.

**Revision Feedback:** When a project needs improvements before approval. Be specific about what needs to change and provide a timeline for resubmission.

**Rejection Feedback:** When a project doesn't meet standards. Provide clear reasons and encourage resubmission after significant revision.

**Encouragement Feedback:** Provided throughout the process to maintain motivation and confidence.

### Feedback Best Practices

**Be Timely:** Provide feedback within 2-3 days of submission when possible. Prompt feedback helps students remember their work and implement suggestions quickly.

**Be Specific:** Use concrete examples from the student work. Avoid vague comments like "good job" or "needs improvement." Instead, reference specific sections or aspects.

**Be Balanced:** Include both strengths and areas for improvement. Students are more receptive to constructive criticism when it's balanced with recognition of their strengths.

**Be Constructive:** Frame feedback as helpful guidance, not criticism. Use language that suggests possibilities and improvements rather than judgment.

**Be Respectful:** Acknowledge the effort and thought students invested in their work. Treat their ideas with respect even when suggesting improvements.

**Be Consistent:** Apply the same standards and feedback approach to all students. Consistency builds fairness and trust.

**Be Growth-Oriented:** Frame feedback in terms of learning and growth. Help students see feedback as an opportunity to improve, not as judgment of their abilities.

### Sample Feedback Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Strong Project, Minor Revisions Needed**

"Excellent work on this project! Your problem identification is clear and well-researched, and your proposed solution is innovative and feasible. Your data analysis effectively supports your conclusions. One area for improvement: your reflection section could be stronger. Can you add more detail about the challenges you encountered and how you overcame them? Also, consider discussing how your solution could be scaled to other communities. With these additions, your project will be ready for the Innovation Hub."

**Scenario 2: Solid Project, Significant Revisions Needed**

"I appreciate the effort you've put into this project. Your team clearly cares about this environmental issue. Your solution addresses the problem, but it needs more development. Specifically: (1) Strengthen your data analysis by including more recent research and statistics, (2) Provide more detail about how your solution would be implemented in practice, and (3) Consider potential challenges and how you would address them. Please revise and resubmit within one week. I'm confident these revisions will strengthen your project significantly."

**Scenario 3: Project Needs Substantial Revision**

"Thank you for submitting this project. While your team's passion for this issue is clear, the project needs substantial revision before it's ready for the Innovation Hub. The main areas needing work are: (1) Your problem identification needs to be more specific and supported by research, (2) Your solution needs more detail and feasibility analysis, and (3) Your data analysis should include more credible sources. I recommend meeting with your team to discuss these areas and creating a revision plan. I'm happy to meet with you to discuss strategies for strengthening the project. Please resubmit when you've addressed these major areas."

## 4. Classroom Integration Strategies

### Connecting the Expo to Your Curriculum

Tomorrow's Earth Expo is designed to complement and enhance your existing curriculum. Here are strategies for effectively integrating the platform into your classroom:

### Before Project Submission

**Introduce the Platform:** Dedicate class time to exploring the platform with students. Show them how to navigate, submit projects, and access resources. Ensure all students understand the project requirements and evaluation criteria.

**Build Background Knowledge:** Use classroom lessons to build the foundational knowledge students need for their projects. Cover relevant environmental science concepts, research methods, and solution development frameworks.

**Teach Research Skills:** Dedicate lessons to finding credible sources, evaluating information quality, and citing sources properly. These skills are essential for strong project work.

**Model Problem-Solving:** Use class examples to model how to identify environmental problems, analyze data, and develop solutions. Think aloud about your decision-making process.

**Facilitate Team Formation:** Help students form teams that balance skills and perspectives. Consider assigning roles (researcher, analyst, writer, presenter) to ensure all team members contribute.

**Establish Timelines:** Create a project timeline with internal deadlines for each phase (problem identification, research, solution development, reflection). Build in time for feedback and revision.

### During Project Development

**Hold Regular Check-ins:** Schedule brief check-ins with each team to monitor progress, answer questions, and provide encouragement. These can be informal conversations or structured meetings.

**Provide Resources:** Share relevant articles, data sources, and examples that support student research. Create a class resource library on the platform.

**Facilitate Peer Review:** Have students review each other's work and provide feedback. This develops critical thinking and helps students learn from their peers.

**Address Challenges:** Help teams overcome obstacles. If a team is struggling with their problem identification, help them refine their focus. If they're having trouble finding data, help them identify sources.

**Celebrate Progress:** Acknowledge and celebrate milestones. When a team completes their research or finalizes their solution, celebrate that progress.

### After Project Submission

**Provide Timely Feedback:** Return feedback within 2-3 days when possible. Use the feedback to guide classroom discussions about effective problem-solving.

**Facilitate Reflection:** Dedicate class time for students to reflect on their learning. Discuss what they learned about environmental issues, problem-solving, and teamwork.

**Celebrate Approvals:** When projects are approved and appear on the Innovation Hub, celebrate with the class. Have students share their projects with the broader school community.

**Analyze Results:** After voting concludes, discuss the results with students. What projects received the most votes? Why? What can we learn from the winning projects?

**Connect to Future Learning:** Use the expo experience to inform future lessons. Reference student projects when teaching related concepts.

### Classroom Discussion Topics

**Environmental Problem-Solving:** Use student projects as case studies for classroom discussions about environmental challenges and solutions. What makes a solution effective? What are the trade-offs?

**Data Literacy:** Discuss how students used data in their projects. What data sources were most useful? How did data analysis inform their decisions?

**Teamwork and Collaboration:** Reflect on team dynamics. What made teams effective? What challenges did they face? How did they overcome them?

**Innovation and Creativity:** Celebrate innovative solutions. What made them creative? How did students balance innovation with feasibility?

**Environmental Citizenship:** Connect projects to broader themes of environmental responsibility and citizenship. How can students apply what they learned to real-world environmental action?

## 5. Using the Admin Dashboard

### Accessing Your Teacher Dashboard

When you log in to the platform as a teacher, you'll see a "Teacher Dashboard" link in the main navigation. Click this to access your dashboard where you manage student projects and provide feedback.

### Dashboard Overview

Your teacher dashboard provides several key views and functions:

**My Projects:** View all projects submitted by your students. Projects are organized by status (Draft, Submitted, Under Review, Approved, Rejected). Click on any project to view details and provide feedback.

**Pending Reviews:** See projects awaiting your review and feedback. This helps you prioritize which projects to review first.

**Class Analytics:** View class-level statistics including total projects submitted, average rubric scores, category distribution, and student participation rates.

**Feedback History:** View a history of feedback you've provided and student responses to your feedback.

**Resources:** Access the Resources section to share materials with your students.

### Reviewing and Approving Projects

**To Review a Project:**

1. Click on the project from your project list
2. Review the project details, description, team members, and any submitted files
3. Read through the student's work carefully
4. Consider the rubric criteria and how well the project meets each dimension
5. Provide feedback using the feedback form
6. Assign a rubric score for each dimension
7. Make an approval or rejection decision

**To Approve a Project:**

1. After reviewing the project and providing feedback, click "Approve Project"
2. Add any final comments
3. Click "Submit Approval"
4. The project will appear on the Innovation Hub and be eligible for voting

**To Request Revisions:**

1. If the project needs improvement, click "Request Revisions"
2. Clearly specify what needs to be revised
3. Provide a deadline for resubmission
4. Click "Send Revision Request"
5. Students will receive a notification and can resubmit the revised project

**To Reject a Project:**

1. If the project doesn't meet standards, click "Reject Project"
2. Provide clear reasons for rejection
3. Encourage the student to revise and resubmit
4. Click "Submit Rejection"
5. Students can revise and resubmit after addressing your feedback

### Providing Feedback Through the Platform

**To Provide Feedback:**

1. Click on the project you want to review
2. Scroll to the feedback section
3. Click "Add Feedback"
4. Type your feedback using the framework provided (strengths, areas for improvement, suggestions, reflective questions)
5. Score each rubric dimension
6. Click "Submit Feedback"
7. Students will receive a notification that you've provided feedback

**Feedback Best Practices in the Platform:**

- Use the provided rubric dimensions to structure your feedback
- Be specific and reference the student work
- Provide actionable suggestions
- Include both strengths and areas for improvement
- Maintain a supportive, encouraging tone
- Proofread before submitting

### Monitoring Student Progress

**Class Analytics:**

Your dashboard shows class-level analytics including:
- Total projects submitted
- Percentage of projects approved
- Average rubric scores by dimension
- Category distribution (how many projects in each category)
- Student participation rates
- Timeline of submissions

Use this data to identify trends and areas where your class might need additional support.

**Individual Student Progress:**

You can view each student's project status and progress. This helps you identify students who might be struggling and need additional support.

## 6. Best Practices & Tips for Success

### Creating an Effective Learning Environment

**Set Clear Expectations:** From the beginning, clearly communicate project requirements, evaluation criteria, timelines, and your expectations for student work. Use the rubric to help students understand what excellence looks like.

**Provide Structure:** Break the project into phases with internal deadlines. This helps students manage the work and prevents last-minute scrambling.

**Support Diverse Learners:** Recognize that students have different strengths and learning styles. Provide multiple ways for students to engage with the project (research, analysis, creative solution development, presentation).

**Encourage Collaboration:** Foster a classroom culture where students support each other, share resources, and learn from their peers. Peer feedback and peer learning are powerful tools.

**Celebrate Effort:** Recognize and celebrate student effort, progress, and achievements. This builds confidence and motivation.

### Maximizing Student Learning

**Connect to Real-World Impact:** Help students see how their projects could create real-world change. Invite environmental professionals to speak to your class. Take field trips to see environmental challenges and solutions in action.

**Teach Critical Thinking:** Use the project as an opportunity to develop critical thinking skills. Ask students to evaluate sources, consider multiple perspectives, and think about unintended consequences.

**Emphasize Process Over Product:** While the final project is important, emphasize the learning process. Help students see that how they approach problems is as important as the solution they develop.

**Build Scientific Literacy:** Use the project to develop students' understanding of scientific concepts and methods. Help them see how science informs environmental decision-making.

**Develop Professional Skills:** Use the project to develop skills students will need in environmental careers: research, analysis, communication, teamwork, and problem-solving.

### Managing Common Challenges

**Challenge 1: Unequal Team Contribution**

**Solution:** Establish clear team roles and responsibilities. Have students complete individual contributions logs. Check in regularly with teams to ensure all members are participating. If problems persist, consider reassigning students to different teams or having them work individually.

**Challenge 2: Students Choosing Problems That Are Too Broad or Too Vague**

**Solution:** Help students narrow their focus. Ask guiding questions: "What specific aspect of this issue are you addressing?" "Who is affected by this problem?" "What geographic area are you focusing on?" Provide examples of well-defined problems.

**Challenge 3: Insufficient Research or Data**

**Solution:** Dedicate class time to teaching research skills and identifying credible sources. Create a resource library with links to useful databases and websites. Provide feedback early so students have time to find additional sources.

**Challenge 4: Solutions That Are Unrealistic or Unfeasible**

**Solution:** Help students balance innovation with feasibility. Ask them to research similar projects and assess what resources, skills, and time their solution would require. Encourage them to start small and think about scalability.

**Challenge 5: Students Struggling with Reflection**

**Solution:** Provide reflection prompts and examples. Have students complete reflection journals throughout the project. Facilitate class discussions about learning and growth. Model reflective thinking in your own practice.

### Grading and Assessment Considerations

**Use the Rubric Consistently:** Apply the rubric consistently across all students and projects. This ensures fairness and helps students understand expectations.

**Weight Dimensions Appropriately:** Consider whether all rubric dimensions should be weighted equally or if some should count more. Adjust weights based on your learning goals.

**Provide Opportunities for Revision:** Allow students to revise and resubmit projects. This reinforces that learning is iterative and that mistakes are opportunities for improvement.

**Balance Individual and Team Grades:** Consider how to grade team projects fairly. You might grade the overall project as a team grade and also assess individual contributions.

**Document Your Feedback:** Keep records of feedback you provide and how students respond. This helps you track progress and provides documentation for grading.

### Professional Development

**Learn from Colleagues:** Connect with other teachers using the platform. Share strategies, challenges, and solutions. Collaborate on classroom integration approaches.

**Explore Student Work:** Regularly review student projects to understand what students are learning and where they might need support. Use student work to inform your teaching.

**Reflect on Your Practice:** Regularly reflect on what's working in your classroom and what could be improved. Adjust your approach based on student needs and feedback.

**Stay Current:** Keep up with environmental education best practices and innovations. The field of environmental education is constantly evolving.
